Collaborative Learning vs Cooperative Learning

Collaborative Learning:

  • Focuses on group participation and collective problem-solving.
  • Students self-organize: they share ideas, work together without predetermined roles, and build knowledge through open dialogue.
  • The process is flexible and learner-centered, emphasizing creativity, critical thinking, and mutual understanding.
  • Teachers act mainly as facilitators, guiding discussion and supporting student autonomy.

Cooperative Learning:

  • Involves structured teamwork with clearly assigned roles and specific tasks.
  • Each student is accountable for a distinct part of the work, ensuring that all contributions are integral to group success.
  • Emphasizes organization, time management, and positive interdependence.
  • Teachers structure the activity and monitor progress, often providing explicit instructions and evaluation criteria

Why Discuss Cooperative Learning (in team context)

  • Cooperative learning groups are more performant than individual members or traditional classroom groups
  • Importance of teamwork in industry and improving psychological health
  • Collaborative learning is not a zero-sum game
  • Emphasizes learning in relation to other students and ties everything together

Types of Groups

Source: https://www.keka.com/glossary/group-dynamics

  • Informal groups: ad hoc, task-based, and disbanded quickly
  • Formal groups: assigned by instructor, may have some thought put into assignment, and last for a lesson to a term
  • Cooperative base groups: formal, assigned by instructor, designed to last for years, and focus on interdependence and long-term relationships

Characteristics of Cooperative Learning Groups

  • Heterogeneous experience brings more than the sum of individual parts
  • Improves understanding through debate and discussion
  • Makes learners process and synthesize information instead of rote memorization
  • Fosters motivation, and emotional and social growth

Care and Feeding of Cooperative Learning Groups

  • Avoid student-selected groups as they tend to be homogeneous
  • Use random or instructor-assigned grouping methods to promote diversity
  • Keep group size small (max 6, ideally 4 or less) to encourage interaction and prevent hiding
  • Teach cooperative skills: forming, functioning, formulating, and fermenting

Cooperative Skills

  • Forming: organizing the group and establishing norms
  • Functioning: managing the group's efforts and maintaining effective working relationships
  • Formulating: providing mental processes for deeper understanding and stimulating higher quality reasoning strategies
  • Fermenting: stimulating reconceptualization, cognitive conflict, and communication of rationales

The Problem

Students are naturally avoidant.

  • Students may lack awareness of 21st-century skills for effective group formation.
  • 'Student-formed' groups tend to be homogeneous
  • It's a challenge to get students to collaborate or cooperate
  • Forcing group behavior may be seen as unconventional
  • Incentivizing students to collaborate and connect, is a natural solution

A Solution: Assignment Configurations

Assignment Configurations involve structuring assignments to promote collaboration and optimize learning outcomes.

  • Involves incentive alignment for practical implementation.
  • Consists of Assignment Bundles and Assignment Trading
  • Promotes high-performance group behaviors.
  • Design can include characteristics like due dates and technology specifications.

Assignment Bundles

Assignment Bundles involve grouping sets of assignments with specific criteria to encourage participation and collaboration.

  • Grouping assignments to promote collaboration
  • Assignments have components with specific criteria
  • Encourages students to trade components to optimize schedules and specialties
  • Promotes familiarity among students creating effective groups

An Example

An Example involves a bundle of 9 assignments with 3 components each, traded among group members to complete the bundle.

  • Students assigned a bundle of 9 assignments, each with 3 components
  • Students must trade components at specific intervals
  • Final grouping of components can be relevant to course material
  • For example, on completion each component recieves a letter
  • Instructor evaluates collective work against a 'secret key'
