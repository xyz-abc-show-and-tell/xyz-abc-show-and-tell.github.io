AI Ethics and Bias in Education: What Teachers Need to Know

May 09, 2025
Education

As artificial intelligence becomes increasingly embedded in educational software, teachers need to understand the ethical implications and potential biases of these technologies. AI systems promise efficiency and personalization but may inadvertently perpetuate existing inequities in education.

Algorithmic bias occurs when “an algorithm encodes the biases present in society, producing predictions or inferences that are clearly discriminatory towards specific groups” (Baker & Hawn, 2021). In educational contexts, this can manifest from biased student performance predictions to inequitable resource allocation.

Recent research provides alarming evidence of bias in educational algorithms. Gándara et al., 2023, found that algorithms used to predict student success produced false negatives for 19% of Black and 21% of Latin students. Dr. Denisa Gándara noted, “It is essential for institutional actors to understand how models perform for specific groups. Our study indicates that models perform better for students categorized as white and Asian.”

UNESCO’s Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (2021) emphasizes human rights, transparency, and fairness in AI applications, stating that “AI systems should be auditable and traceable” with appropriate oversight mechanisms.

The Institute for Ethical AI in Education (2023) highlights specific risks including how “AI systems can perpetuate and amplify existing biases and discrimination, leading to unfair treatment of certain groups of learners.”

Kizilcec and Lee (2020) suggest that fairness in education requires attention at three critical stages: measurement (data input), model learning (algorithm), and action (presentation of output).

Practical suggestions for teachers include:

  1. Critically evaluating AI tools before classroom implementation
  2. Monitoring how different student groups perform with AI-based technologies
  3. Teaching students to recognize potential biases in AI systems
  4. Maintaining human oversight of all algorithmic recommendations

As Jessica Rowland Williams cautions, “Algorithms are only as informed as the programmers and developers who design them… If an algorithm routinely places a student in a learning track that doesn’t align with their learning needs, then this could ultimately hinder growth” (Williams, 2023).

By developing AI literacy and ethical awareness, teachers can harness the benefits of educational AI while protecting students from potential harm.
